About this list

How the 166 Steam hidden gems were chosen, and what the data means.

What counts as a “hidden gem” Steam game here?

A game qualifies if it has 2,000+ reviews that are 95%+ positive, is priced $20 or under, and has a genuinely small audience (roughly 20,000–200,000 owners). Highly rated, cheap, and still under the radar. 175 games cleared the raw filter; 9 were removed for corrupted or duplicate owner data (Steam mega-hits mislabeled as tiny), leaving 166 real hidden gems.

How were these games found?

With SQL. I queried a public Steam dataset of about 125,000 games, applied the review, rating, price, and audience thresholds above, ranked the survivors, and hand-checked the outliers. The full methodology, the exact SQL, and the data-cleaning story are in the companion GitHub repository.

How many games were analyzed?

125,000 titles were in the dataset. After filtering and cleaning, 166 met every criterion and made the list.

Are these games free or paid?

Most are paid but cheap ($20 or under); a few are free-to-play. Use the “Free only” and “Paid picks” filters on the page. Every card links straight to the game’s Steam store page.

What’s the data source, and how current is it?

The Steam Games Dataset by FronkonGames (published on Kaggle). Review counts and ratings reflect that dataset snapshot.

Who made this, and how should I cite it?

Built by Michael Nocito, a data analyst. You can cite it as “Steam Hidden Gems (Michael Nocito, 2026), michaelnocito.github.io/steam-hidden-gems-list”. The underlying review and ownership figures come from the FronkonGames Steam dataset.
